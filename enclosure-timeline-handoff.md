# Handoff: "Mara and Tomas" — a walk through ten thousand years of watching

## What this is

A single-page, horizontally scrolling interactive timeline. Two figures — Mara and Tomas — walk left to right through twenty-five stops spanning foraging bands to the present. At every stop, the same three-line ledger is answered: **who watches**, **what is fenced**, **what is theirs**. The repetition of the ledger is the argument: the reader watches the answers mutate while the questions never change.

This expands a working prototype (a simple 8-panel scroller). The build should treat the prototype as a sketch, not a constraint.

## The intellectual architecture (read before building — it drives every design decision)

1. **There is no golden age of privacy anywhere on the line.** The couple is never unwatched in ten thousand years. What changes is the *geometry* of the gaze (mutual → vertical → paid-and-bounded → boundless) and what the gaze is *measuring for* (norms → taxes → output → the self).
2. **The private sphere is a brief artifact, not a permanent achievement.** It appears late (roughly stops 15–21), manufactured out of two industrial byproducts — bounded time and personal wages — and it is being enclosed at the far end of the line. The design should make its brevity *visible*: on the minimap, the stretch where "what is theirs" is substantive should read as a short bright interval on a long line.
3. **The gender lag is a measurement instrument, not a subplot.** Her ledger lags his at almost every stop — household privacy is his millennia before the personal wage is hers. Wherever their answers diverge, the ledger splits into two rows (his / hers). The divergence and its eventual convergence must be scannable across the whole timeline.
4. **Transitions carry mechanism, not just chronology.** Between acts, narrow connective panels explain *how* one regime became the next (the ratchet, the assessor, the roof, the remainder, the inversion). These are the nuance the prototype lacked. The figures should move through transitions differently — faster walk cadence, compressed panel width — so eras feel like rooms and transitions feel like corridors.
5. **The ending is unresolved on purpose.** The final panel poses an open question and does not answer it. No summary panel after it.

## Content payload

All copy below is final draft quality — use verbatim or lightly trimmed for space. Scholarly anchors are for optional footnote/tooltip UI, not for display in the ledger. Vignettes are in third person, present tense.

### Act I — The watched commons

**Stop 1 · Era · Before 12,000 BCE · The band**
Vignette: They move with the season. Every meal, quarrel, and courtship happens in full view of thirty people who share the fire — and who are watched by them in return. The record of a life is gossip, and gossip decays.
Ledger — Watches: everyone, mutually; the watchers live under the same norms. Fenced: nothing. Theirs: seclusion of the body; little else, and little need.
Anchor: Sahlins, *Stone Age Economics*; Moore, *Privacy: Studies in Social and Cultural History*.
Prop: campfire, ring of stones.

**Transition T1 · The cold snap (c. 12,900–11,700 BCE)**
Mechanism line: The Younger Dryas thins the wild stands. Planting begins as a hedge against a thin winter — an insurance policy, not a way of life.

**Stop 2 · Era · 12,000–9,500 BCE · The hedge**
Vignette: They still hunt and still gather, but now there is a stand of wheat they return to, and a stone house near it worth returning to. Sitting still comes before farming, not after.
Ledger — Watches: the band, as always. Fenced: one stand of grain, informally. Theirs: unchanged — the trade has not yet presented its bill.
Anchor: Natufian sedentism; Bar-Yosef.
Prop: wild wheat beside a round stone hut.

**Stop 3 · Era · 9,500–5,000 BCE · The long ambiguity**
Vignette: Four thousand years in which their descendants farm a little, forage a lot, and move when it suits them. Domesticated seed in hand, they decline the package for a hundred and fifty generations. The choice, while it lasts, is real.
Ledger — Watches: household elders; the kin group. Fenced: plots, seasonally, loosely. Theirs: mobility — the power to walk away, still intact.
Anchor: Scott, *Against the Grain* (the four-millennia gap); Graeber & Wengrow, *The Dawn of Everything* (deliberate refusal, "play farming").
Prop: a field on one side of the panel, open country on the other.

**Transition T2 · The ratchet**
Mechanism line: Sedentary grain-eaters birth children closer together. Numbers rise to what cultivation supports; the wild landscape can no longer carry them back. Each generation inherits a commitment no generation made.

**Stop 4 · Era · c. 5,000 BCE · The trap closed**
Vignette: Mara's grandmother could name the plants of three valleys. Mara knows one valley and one crop. Nobody chose this on any particular day, and there is no particular day on which it can be unchosen.
Ledger — Watches: the household head, whose granary it is. Fenced: land, plot by plot; the first enclosure complete. Theirs: whatever the head of the household allows.
Her line: her labor moves indoors and out of sight — grinding, weaving, bearing. The demographic ratchet runs through her body.
Anchor: Boserup; Cohen & Armelagos, *Paleopathology at the Origins of Agriculture* (the skeletal bill: shorter, sicker, worked harder).
Prop: granary, quern-stone.

### Act II — The legible subject

**Transition T3 · The assessor arrives**
Mechanism line: Stored surplus attracts organization the way a granary attracts mice. Grain — visible, simultaneous, storable, divisible — is the only crop a tax man can count. The state is built on the one plant that can be audited.

**Stop 5 · Era · c. 3,100 BCE · The grain state**
Vignette: An assessor counts their harvest onto a clay tablet; writing is born as accounting, not literature. Tomas owes the palace a season of hauling stone, Mara owes it cloth. The wall keeps raiders out — and keeps the two of them in.
Ledger — Watches: the palace, from above; the first vertical gaze in human history. Fenced: the harvest, by tax; the body, by corvée. Theirs: nothing the scribe can see.
Anchor: Scott, *Against the Grain*; *Seeing Like a State* (legibility as the precondition of extraction).
Prop: ziggurat, tablet, wall.

**Stop 6 · Era · 3,000–1,000 BCE · Flight and collapse**
Vignette: Cousins slip away to the hills where the assessor does not go, and are recorded by the palace as barbarians. When the state collapses — they collapse often — the chroniclers mourn a dark age. From below it can feel like a long exhale.
Ledger — Watches: the palace, where its reach holds; nobody, past the frontier. Fenced: less each year the frontier holds. Theirs: the periphery — exit as the first civil liberty.
Anchor: Scott on collapse-as-relief; the barbarian frontier as safety valve.
Prop: a wall with a gap in it; hills beyond.

**Stop 7 · Era · c. 400 BCE – 200 CE · The classical household**
Vignette: The household is now legally private — but *privative*: the realm of necessity, hidden from the public square where free men appear to one another. The privacy belongs to the household as a jurisdiction, and the jurisdiction belongs to its head.
Ledger — Watches (his): the city, when he enters it; his own house answers to him alone. Watches (hers): him. Fenced: the oikos, as his domain. Theirs (his): a private realm, in the old privative sense. Theirs (hers): nothing — she lives *inside* his privacy, as its object.
Anchor: Arendt, *The Human Condition* (public/private in the polis); the paterfamilias.
Prop: courtyard house, closed door.

### Act III — The mutual village

**Stop 8 · Era · c. 1200 · Village and manor**
Vignette: The house has one room; family, servants, and animals sleep in it, and nothing done there is unknown by morning. The manor court tracks who gleans, who moves, who marries. Neighbors testify about their marriage in the church court — which means neighbors were watching closely enough to testify.
Ledger — Watches: all neighbors, horizontally; the manor court, from the hall. Fenced: land, by the lord; movement, by the manor roll. Theirs: the household — which is to say, his.
Anchor: manor and church court records; Barrington Moore.
Prop: one-room house, church behind.

**Stop 9 · Era · 1215 → · The confessor**
Vignette: Once a year, by law of the Church, each of them kneels and produces an account of the interior itself. The West is learning to examine the self by being required to report it.
Ledger — Watches: the confessor — the first audit of thought. Fenced: sin, catalogued. Theirs: the unconfessed remainder, at the price of the soul.
Anchor: Fourth Lateran Council; Foucault, *History of Sexuality* vol. 1 (the confessional as the engine of self-production).
Prop: confessional screen.

**Stop 10 · Era · 1300s–1600s · Charivari and reputation**
Vignette: When a marriage offends the village, the village arrives at night with pots and horns to say so. Reputation is the currency, everyone holds an account, and the ledger is kept in memory — which softens, forgives, and dies.
Ledger — Watches: the community, enforcing norms it also lives under. Fenced: conduct, by shame. Theirs: the mercy of forgetting — the record decays by design.
Anchor: charivari / rough music literature; Mayer-Schönberger, *Delete* (forgetting as the historical default).
Prop: pots, horns, lantern light.

**Stop 11 · Era · c. 1650 · The tithingman**
Vignette: In Massachusetts it is illegal to live alone; solitary persons are placed into well-governed families. A neighbor is appointed to inspect ten households — theirs among them — for idleness, disorder, and absence from worship. The family is the instrument of order, and the state audits the families.
Ledger — Watches: the tithingman, the minister, the village — vertically *and* horizontally at once. Fenced: solitude itself, by statute. Theirs: nothing the selectmen would recognize.
Anchor: Flaherty, *Privacy in Colonial New England*.
Prop: meetinghouse, ledger book.

### Act IV — The enclosed hour

**Transition T4 · The fences (1500–1800)**
Mechanism line: The commons are enclosed, parcel by parcel, act by act of Parliament. People who once had a landscape now have only their hours to sell — which pushes them toward anyone who will buy hours.

**Stop 12 · Era · 1700s · Putting-out**
Vignette: The merchant leaves wool at their cottage and collects cloth. He cannot see how they work, when they work, or whether the wool all comes back — and it maddens him. Work interpenetrates with life; there is no boundary because no one has purchased one side of it yet.
Ledger — Watches: the merchant, inspecting output only — the process is invisible to him. Fenced: materials, imperfectly. Theirs: pace, method, Saint Monday — the whole rhythm of the day.
Anchor: Marglin, "What Do Bosses Do?"; Thompson on task-orientation.
Prop: cottage loom, wool sacks.

**Transition T5 · The roof**
Mechanism line: The early factory often uses the same machines as the cottage, gathered under one roof. What the roof purchases is observation. The factory is a surveillance technology before it is a production technology.

**Stop 13 · Era · 1790–1840 · The mill**
Vignette: Tomas weaves beneath the overseer's raised desk, in a long room built for unbroken sightlines, paced by a machine that reports him by slowing. Mara lives under the boardinghouse keeper's curfew, her church attendance recorded, her conduct a condition of employment. The bell owns twelve hours of their day — and in owning twelve, it has defined a remainder. Leave without a certificate of honorable discharge and no mill in the region will hire you: the corporations circulate names.
Ledger — Watches: paid overseers and keepers — asymmetric, purposive, bounded by the gate. Fenced: their time, purchased by the clock; their reputations, held in a cross-employer file. Theirs: the unsold hours — a remainder appears, unnamed and unfortified.
Her line: she was recruited *because* she is unmarried — the only legal status in which her wages are her own. The moral apparatus around her is the labor-supply mechanism.
Anchor: Thompson, "Time, Work-Discipline, and Industrial Capitalism"; Dublin, *Women at Work*; Pollard; the Waltham-Lowell regulations; the blacklist and discharge certificate.
Prop: mill with clock tower, bell, boardinghouse row.

**Stop 14 · Era · 1834–1848 · The turnouts**
Vignette: The boardinghouse built to watch her has concentrated her with eighty women who now know each other well enough to act together. The petitions and turnouts are organized through the very architecture that was meant to supervise. Mutual visibility cuts in both directions.
Ledger — Watches: the corporation — and now the women watch each other, enforcing strike pledges through the same channels. Fenced: still their time; the ten-hour day is the demand. Theirs: solidarity — the surveillance system's unplanned product.
Anchor: Dublin on the 1834/1836 turnouts and the ten-hour petition campaigns; the *Voice of Industry* vs. the *Lowell Offering*.
Prop: petition sheet, gathered figures.

**Transition T6 · The remainder**
Mechanism line: Once time is commodified, the unsold portion becomes property. The first generation is taught the clock; the second organizes to shorten the day; the third demands overtime rates. Workers learn the lesson of time-is-money and turn it back on the masters.

**Stop 15 · Era · 1866–1890s · Eight hours for what we will**
Vignette: Eight hours for work, eight for rest, eight for what we will — a demand for a private sphere, stated as such. Not recovery so the body can work again: a zone of unaccountable time, purpose unspecified, justification owed to no one. It is not granted. It is taken, strike by strike.
Ledger — Watches: the boss — until the whistle. After it, remarkably little. Fenced: time, now openly contested. Theirs: evenings, won; "what we will," named.
Anchor: Rosenzweig, *Eight Hours for What We Will*; the shorter-hours movement.
Prop: factory whistle, evening street.

**Stop 16 · Era · 1848–1882 · Her wage becomes hers** *(gender-lag stop — render the split prominently)*
Vignette: Under coverture, a married woman's earnings belong to her husband; the Married Women's Property Acts arrive piecemeal across four decades. Mara's wage packet — hers at the mill only because she was single — becomes hers in marriage too, on paper, state by state. One operative's letter home had already said the whole thing: *I have my own money now.*
Ledger — Watches: unchanged. Fenced: unchanged. Theirs (his): established for a generation. Theirs (hers): arriving now, by statute, decades behind — privacy is downstream of the purse.
Anchor: coverture and the Married Women's Property Acts; Woolf, *A Room of One's Own* as the theory of this stop, written seventy years later: independent income plus a defensible room as the material prerequisites of an inner life.
Prop: wage packet, a door with a key.

### Act V — The invented private

**Stop 17 · Era · c. 1890 · Privacy invented**
Vignette: The corridor spreads through ordinary houses — a way to reach a room without walking through other rooms. Separate bedrooms, the diary, the novel, silent reading. And in the *Harvard Law Review*, two lawyers alarmed by the snap camera name a new right: to be let alone. The mill girl in the boardinghouse and the mill owner's daughter with her locked writing desk are contemporaries — privacy is being assembled in one house while surveillance is assembled in the other, funded by the same dividends.
Ledger — Watches: the press and the camera — the new intruders that provoke the new right. Fenced: nothing new; something is being *un*-fenced. Theirs: interiority — architectural, literary, and now legal.
Anchor: Warren & Brandeis, "The Right to Privacy" (1890); Habermas, *Structural Transformation*; Ariès / *A History of Private Life*; Watt on the novel.
Prop: corridor doorways, a diary, a box camera.

**Stop 18 · Era · 1900s–1920s · Privacy in public**
Vignette: The tenement is too crowded to hold the new privacy, so she takes it outdoors: the dance hall, the nickelodeon, the amusement park — anonymous commercial rooms where no neighbor reports to anyone's mother. The bourgeois version of privacy is architectural; hers is circulatory. Same function, opposite geometry.
Ledger — Watches: no one who knows her — the crowd as concealment. Fenced: leisure, by the ticket price. Theirs: autonomy through anonymity — privacy without walls.
Anchor: Peiss, *Cheap Amusements*; Marwick & boyd's "context collapse" names, a century later, exactly what these separated audiences protected.
Prop: dance hall marquee, streetlamp.

**Stop 19 · Era · 1914 · The employer reaches over**
Vignette: Ford's five-dollar day is paid in full only to workers whose homes pass inspection. Investigators visit the house, interview the wife and neighbors, check the bank book, evaluate sobriety, cleanliness, and boarders. The wage that created the private sphere is leveraged to audit it. The boundary, from the day it was drawn, is a contested front.
Ledger — Watches: the Sociological Department, across the line the whistle was supposed to close. Fenced: domestic conduct, as a wage condition. Theirs: the evenings — defended, inspected, kept anyway.
Anchor: Ford Sociological Department; welfare capitalism literature.
Prop: inspector at a front door, clipboard.

**Stop 20 · Era · 1950s · The wired home**
Vignette: A room of their own at last, behind suburban walls no tithingman inspects — the architectural peak of ordinary privacy in the whole ten thousand years. Inside it, a television sells their reclaimed evening back to them, one program at a time. The leisure market moved in the day the lease was signed.
Ledger — Watches: remarkably little — the historical minimum. Fenced: free time, by the market that colonized it. Theirs: the walls, the door, the diary — and a rented pastime.
Anchor: postwar suburbanization; the commercialization-of-leisure literature.
Prop: house with television antenna, glowing screen.

**Stop 21 · Era · 1965–1970 · The file**
Vignette: In offices they will never visit, bureaus assemble folders on their credit, their addresses, their habits of payment. When Congress regulates it in 1970, the law's premise concedes the fact: a paper self now exists, consulted by strangers, correctable only by petition. The profile's ancestor is a filing cabinet.
Ledger — Watches: the bureau — remote, commercial, consulted at decision points. Fenced: reputation, formalized into a record that no longer decays. Theirs: a right to *inspect* the file — the first admission that the file, not the person, is what institutions read.
Anchor: Fair Credit Reporting Act (1970); the pre-digital dossier.
Prop: filing cabinets receding in rows.

### Act VI — The boundless watcher

**Transition T7 · The inversion**
Mechanism line: Transparency entered modern life as a demand on institutions — sunlight as disinfectant. Over three decades it migrates: from a duty of the powerful to a virtue of persons, while the powerful acquire unprecedented opacity. The platform knows you completely and is itself a trade secret.

**Stop 22 · Era · 2004–2012 · The exhibition**
Vignette: No one compels the disclosures; the architecture invites them, and the invitation feels like friendship. Mara's separated audiences — work, family, the dance hall — collapse into a single feed read by all of them at once. The confessional never closed; it acquired a business model.
Ledger — Watches: everyone again — but through a commercial pane, permanently recorded. Fenced: attention, sociality, the presentation of self. Theirs: the account settings — contextual walls, dissolving.
Anchor: Han, *The Transparency Society* (voluntary self-exposure); Marwick & boyd (context collapse); Nissenbaum, *Privacy in Context* (privacy as appropriate flow, not secrecy).
Prop: glowing rectangles held by a crowd.

**Stop 23 · Era · 2010s · The profile governs**
Vignette: Somewhere, a version of Mara she cannot inspect applies for her loan, sets her premium, ranks her application. Village reputation could be answered at the well; a score cannot be answered at all. The record no longer softens, forgives, or dies — remembering has become the default, for the first time in the history of the species.
Ledger — Watches: systems, continuously; no watcher who can be confronted, shamed, or outlived. Fenced: the self — behavior, attention, the traces of a life, assembled and owned elsewhere. Theirs: the natural person — retaining the rituals while the data body conducts the business.
Anchor: Cheney-Lippold, *We Are Data*; Rouvroy & Berns on algorithmic governmentality; Mayer-Schönberger, *Delete*; Pasquale, *The Black Box Society*.
Prop: a second, translucent pair of figures standing apart from Mara and Tomas — the doubles. (This is the one panel where the walking figures acquire shadows that do not match their movement.)

**Stop 24 · Era · 2020 · The gateless day**
Vignette: Tomas works from the bedroom while the laptop counts keystrokes and samples his screen. Work has returned to the cottage — putting-out restored — except the merchant can now see everything the roof was invented to reveal. The factory pulled work out of the home because watching required co-presence; the watching no longer does, so the home gets the work back, observed. There is no gate for the whistle to close.
Ledger — Watches: the employer, inside the house, on a channel that never closes. Fenced: the boundary itself — the founding concession of the wage relation, quietly repealed. Theirs: an unbounded interpenetration of work and life, as before the clock — but paced by metrics instead of seasons.
Anchor: Marglin, inverted; workplace-monitoring literature post-2020; Thompson's task-orientation, degraded echo.
Prop: bed, laptop, small green camera light.

**Stop 25 · Era · Now · The open question**
Vignette: They encrypt some messages. She keeps a second account under a name that is not hers; he pays cash for small things, out of an instinct he does not fully articulate. Every earlier enclosure eventually produced a counter-movement that partially re-embedded what had been taken — the factory acts, the eight-hour day, the union. Whether these small refusals are that movement's beginning, or merely its dance hall, is not yet knowable — and this timeline does not pretend to know it.
Ledger — Watches: everything. Fenced: the last commons — interiority itself, mid-enclosure. Theirs: the question.
Anchor: Polanyi's double movement, as open problem.
Prop: the two figures at the panel's edge, facing the unrendered space beyond the last frame. No ground line ahead of them.

## Interaction and motion spec

- **Horizontal scroll** with scroll-snap on era panels; transitions (T1–T7) are narrow corridor panels (roughly 40% era width) that do *not* snap — the reader passes through them.
- **The figures** are position-sticky within the scroller (or fixed within a wrapper), standing on a continuous ground line. Walking animation is **scroll-driven**: cadence proportional to scroll velocity; idle when still; a faster cadence through transition corridors. Respect `prefers-reduced-motion` — static figures, instant panel changes.
- **The ground line is the signature element.** Its texture changes per act and the change is visible mid-transition: bare earth → furrow → paving stones → mill floorboards → asphalt → backlit glass. In the final panel the ground line stops before the panel edge.
- **Parallax, three layers:** far props (0.4× scroll rate), near props (0.8×), ground/figures (1×). Subtle — atmosphere, not spectacle.
- **The ledger** renders identically at every stop (same position, same three labels) so the eye can scan it like a registry. Where his/hers diverge, the row splits into two lines with a small marker; the marker's presence/absence across the timeline is itself data.
- **Minimap** across the top or bottom: 25 ticks grouped by act, current position indicated, clickable. Overlay on the minimap a thin band showing where "what is theirs" is substantive — making the brevity of the private interval visible at a glance (architecture point 2).
- **Keyboard:** arrow keys move stop-by-stop; visible focus states throughout.
- **Deep links:** each stop gets a URL hash (`#the-mill`, `#the-file`).
- **Optional footnote layer:** an unobtrusive control per panel revealing the scholarly anchors.

## Design direction

- Ground the palette in the subject's materials, not a default theme: clay tablet, ledger paper, indigo ink, wheat, iron. Suggested starting tokens — parchment `#EFE7D6`, tablet clay `#A9744F`, iron `#3A3A38`, indigo ink `#2B3A67`, wheat `#C9A24B`, register red (sparingly, for the fenced line) `#8C2F2F`. Provide a dark variant deliberately; do not let the palette invert automatically.
- Type: a transitional serif with real historical character for era titles and vignettes; a humanist sans for UI; **a monospaced face for the ledger** — the three questions set like entries in a registry is the typographic argument of the piece. Two weights per face maximum.
- Props are flat, silhouette-style SVG — consistent stroke and fill treatment across all twenty-five stops so ten millennia read as one visual language. No skeuomorphism, no gradients, no drop shadows.
- Copy discipline: vignettes ≤ 3 sentences; ledger values ≤ 12 words per line; transition mechanism lines are a single sentence.
- Spend boldness once (the ground line + the registry type); keep everything else quiet.

## Technical notes

- Vanilla HTML/CSS/JS or a light framework — author's choice; no heavy dependencies required. Scroll-driven animation via `scroll` events with rAF throttling, or CSS scroll-driven animations where supported with a JS fallback.
- Content should live in a single structured data file (JSON or JS module), one object per stop: `{ id, act, kind: "era" | "transition", dateLabel, title, vignette, ledger: { watches, fenced, theirs, theirsHers? }, herLine?, anchors[], prop }` — so stops can be added or reordered without touching layout code.
- Responsive to mobile: panels become full-viewport-width; figures scale down; minimap collapses to a progress bar with act labels.
- Accessibility: semantic sections per stop, `aria-current` on the active stop, alt text on every prop, full keyboard operability, reduced-motion honored.

## Acceptance criteria

1. All 25 era stops and 7 transition corridors present, with the copy above.
2. Ledger renders in identical position and format at every stop; his/hers splits appear at stops 7, 16, and anywhere else the payload marks divergence.
3. Ground-line texture changes per act and terminates before the edge of the final panel.
4. Figures walk when scrolling, rest when idle, hurry through corridors; reduced-motion respected.
5. Minimap shows the short bright interval of substantive privacy against the full line.
6. Final panel ends on the open question with no summary after it.
7. Keyboard navigation, deep links, and mobile layout all functional.
